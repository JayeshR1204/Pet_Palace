Burmese cat

View Gallary
  • Origin: Myanmar
  • Height: 9 to 13 inches
  • Weight: 3-5 kg
  • Color: chocolate, brown and red
  • Life-Span: 18 to 25 years
  • Personality: Curious, clever, and friendly
  • Scientific Name: Copper Cat


About This Pet

The Burmese cat is a popular breed known for its affectionate nature and striking appearance. Burmese cats have a medium-sized, muscular body with a rounded head and expressive eyes. They have a dense, silky coat that comes in various colors, including sable, champagne, blue, platinum, and more.The breed gained popularity for its unique appearance and loving temperament.Burmese cats have a medium-sized, muscular body with a well-rounded chest and a solid weight. They have a compact build, but they are not as slender as some other cat breeds. Their strong, athletic physique contributes to their agility and grace.The Burmese cat has a short, satin-like coat that lies close to their body. Their coat is often described as having a luxurious feel.


-:    Personality    :-

Burmese cats have a warm and affectionate personality, known for their love of human companionship. They are social, people-oriented, and seek constant physical contact, often curling up in laps or following their owners around the house. Their playful and energetic nature adds a sense of joy to their interactions, while their intelligence and curiosity make them quick learners and eager explorers. With their gentle demeanor and ability to get along with children and other pets, Burmese cats make loving and devoted companions that thrive on attention and form strong bonds with their families. Burmese cats are generally known to get along well with children and other pets. They have a friendly and tolerant nature and are often patient with younger family members.


-:    Grooming    :-

Burmese cats benefit from regular brushing to remove loose hairs and prevent matting. A soft-bristle brush or a rubber grooming mitt can be used to gently brush their coat. Aim for weekly brushing sessions to keep their coat in good condition.Burmese cats typically do not require frequent baths unless they get dirty or have specific skin conditions. However, if you need to bathe your Burmese cat, use a cat-specific shampoo and ensure the water is lukewarm. Take care to rinse thoroughly and dry them well afterward.Regular nail trimming is essential to prevent overgrowth and discomfort. Use cat nail clippers or a grinder to trim their nails, being careful not to cut into the quick. If you're unsure about nail trimming, consult a veterinarian or a professional groomer for guidance.


-:    Care & Maintenance    :-

Provide a balanced and nutritious diet that meets the specific needs of your Burmese cat. Choose high-quality cat food, either commercially prepared or a balanced homemade diet recommended by a veterinarian. Follow the feeding guidelines based on their age, weight, and activity level, and ensure they have access to fresh water at all times. Provide a clean litter box and maintain good hygiene. Burmese cats are generally fastidious and prefer a clean environment. Scoop the litter box daily and change the litter regularly. Oral health is crucial for Burmese cats. Brush their teeth regularly with cat-specific toothbrushes and toothpaste to prevent dental issues. Dental treats or toys designed to promote oral hygiene can also be beneficial.


-:    History    :-

The history of Burmese cats can be traced back to Burma (now Myanmar) in the 1930s. The breed was developed from a small number of cats obtained from Burma by Western travelers and brought to the United States and Britain. The exact origins and the specific cats used in the breeding program are not well-documented, but it is believed that they were descendants of temple cats in Burma. The breed's development began in the United States when a Burmese cat named Wong Mau was imported from Burma to San Francisco in 1930 by Dr. Joseph Thompson. Wong Mau, described as a small, dark brown cat, became the foundation cat for the breed in the United States. She was initially classified as a Siamese cat but was later recognized as a distinct breed.Dr. Thompson began a breeding program with Wong Mau, crossing her with Siamese cats to preserve her unique appearance and develop a breed standard. This marked the establishment of the Burmese breed in the United States. The breed gained recognition and popularity for its striking appearance, warm temperament, and unique coat color.
